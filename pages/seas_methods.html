METHODS

The results of this project have also been used to complement the information generated by the seasonal forecasting group of the Climate Evaluation and Modeling Area at AEMET. Our aim was to study whether statistical downscaling methods offer added value when applied to ECMWF seasonal forecasting models (see Data section for more information). DeepESD method was selected due to its potential as a deep learning methodology and its good results (see Downscaling section for more information) with the objective of downscaling the hindcasts, verifying them by calculating deterministic and probabilistic scores, and finally, downscaling the forecasts as well.

Although pyClim-SDM software is very comprehensive and useful for downscaling global climate models, seasonal model inputs are very specific and do not work well within it. Therefore, deep4downscaling, the Python package that pyClim-SDM uses to configure DeepESD, was applied directly. deep4downscaling enables the execution of statistical downscaling with deep learning through tools for preprocessing climate data, transforming and standardizing variables, building and training deep learning models, evaluating performance metrics, and performing post-processing and bias correction. You can access deep4downscaling at the following link.

For a detailed description of DeepESD and how its methodology works, see the Downscaling section. In this case, the DeepESD model was calculated using predictors obtained from ERA5 and the predictand obtained from CERRA for the period 1997–2016. A leave-one-out (LOO) methodology was used, in which the model is trained by excluding one observation (in this case, one year, to break temporal autocorrelation and avoid overly optimistic results), and then the model is used to predict precisely that excluded observation (the year in question). This avoids overfitting, simulates how the model would perform with "independent" data, and allows for the calculation of reliable metrics. The leave-one-out model was applied to each year of the hindcast (25 members, 1997–2016), and then a global model was applied to the forecast (51 members, 2024 and 2025), both for their October initializations, i.e., for the extended winter.

Once downscaled hindcasts were obtained, they were verified using the following procedure:

  1. Calculation of year-on-year anomalies with respect to hindcast climatology (LOO).

  2. Calculation of year-on-year tercile probabilities.

  3. Calculation of scores. The following scores were used. Spearman's rank correlation coefficient measures model ability to reproduce interannual variability; other correlations were also calculated (Pearson, which is the measure of linear dependence between two quantitative random variables). Area under the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve (AUC) measures how well the model assigns higher probabilities when the predicted tercile actually occurs and evaluates each category individually; an AUC greater than 0.5 indicates predictive ability, while an AUC of 1 indicates perfect discrimination. Ranked probability score (RPS) assesses forecast quality for ordered categories (lower, normal, and upper terciles), compares the predicted cumulative distribution with the observed distribution, and penalizes both category errors and probability assignment errors; an RPS of 0 indicates a perfect forecast.

Once downscaled hindcasts were verified, model forecasts for two years (2024 and 2025) were also downscaled and evaluated by calculating anomalies and tercilial probability.

These calculations were performed for both 3-month and 5-month aggregations.
